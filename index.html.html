Executive Profile

Most organizational dysfunction is structural before it is operational. The symptoms — missed deliverables, poor adoption, compliance exposure, leadership decisions made without adequate information — are downstream effects of systems, governance structures, and operating models that were never designed to scale.

I am an organizational transformation executive. My work is to identify where those structural failures live, design the interventions that address them at the root, and build the operational infrastructure that allows organizations to perform at a consistently higher level. I have done this work inside a nationally recognized federal research enterprise operating at $110M+ in active VA and DOD funding, and externally through ZenStrata Consulting LLC, where I serve as a principal consultant and transformation advisor to organizations that need to build or rebuild their operational foundation.

What I bring to a C-suite or senior leadership context is the ability to hold two things simultaneously: the strategic view that sees where an organization needs to go, and the operational precision that understands what it will actually take to get there. I partner with executive leadership to make that gap navigable — designing the governance structures, portfolio systems, workforce plans, and change management frameworks that translate vision into execution without the organizational wreckage that typically accompanies transformation at scale.

My background spans six years in a complex, multi-stakeholder federal research environment and an active consulting practice serving healthcare, behavioral health, and government clients. The industries I have worked in are different. The organizational problems I solve are not.

Operating as an Executive
Structure is a strategic decision

How an organization is structured — its governance, its intake processes, its reporting architecture — determines what it can and cannot do. I treat structural design as a leadership responsibility, not an administrative one.

Diagnosis before redesign

Transformation efforts fail most often because they address symptoms rather than causes. I invest in accurate organizational diagnosis before recommending any structural intervention — because solutions built on wrong diagnoses are expensive in ways that are hard to recover from.

The C-suite interface is where this work lives

Operational transformation cannot be delegated below the executive level and expected to hold. I am most effective when I have direct access to the leadership making the decisions the transformation depends on — and when I can give them the information they need to make those decisions well.

Sustainability over velocity

The STRATA Framework

Most transformation efforts fail not because the strategy is wrong, but because the organization was never assessed accurately enough to know what it actually needed. The STRATA Framework is a proprietary four-phase organizational diagnostic and redesign methodology developed through ZenStrata Consulting LLC — built to close that gap.

STRATA is designed for organizations that are scaling, restructuring, or underperforming — and need a rigorous, evidence-based starting point before committing to a transformation strategy. It is deployed across healthcare, behavioral health, federal programs, and enterprise operations contexts. Every engagement in this portfolio was shaped by its principles.

01
Scan
Organizational Diagnostic

Map the current state with precision. Identify where structural failures live — in governance, intake, information flow, accountability, or operational design — before recommending any intervention. The presenting problem is assessed separately from the root cause.

02
Target
Strategic Prioritization

Translate diagnostic findings into a prioritized intervention roadmap. Sequence work by risk, capacity, and dependency — not urgency. Align leadership on what will be addressed, in what order, and what tradeoffs that sequencing requires.

03
Redesign
Structural Intervention

Design and implement the governance structures, operating models, workflows, and accountability frameworks that address root causes. Build for adoption from day one — treating behavior change and stakeholder alignment as design constraints, not post-launch communications.

04
Anchor
Capability Transfer

Transfer capability to the organization so that the transformation holds after the engagement ends. Embed performance monitoring, establish governance cadences, and build internal leadership capacity to sustain and extend the work independently.

Governance & Compliance
Governance Fragmentation as Organizational Risk: Redesigning Oversight Across a Distributed Multi-Site Program
The Business Problem

When a multi-site research consortium operates under fragmented, site-specific governance structures, it creates compounding risk: regulatory inconsistencies expose the organization to audit findings, funding bodies lose confidence in oversight integrity, and any single site's non-compliance can jeopardize the entire consortium's standing. For this organization, a major funding transition made that risk acute. Without a unified governance model in place before the transition, the consortium faced the possibility of delayed approvals, sponsor scrutiny, and operational disruption across all participating institutions — at the worst possible moment.

What Was at Stake

Funding continuity, regulatory standing, and the consortium's ability to execute across sites without inconsistency creating downstream legal or compliance exposure during a high-scrutiny transition period.

The Intervention

Served as project lead for the governance transition. Assessed each site's existing oversight model to surface gaps and dependencies, designed a phased centralization roadmap aligned to funding milestones, and drove executive and site-level alignment on roles, escalation paths, and shared standards — before execution began.

Why It Mattered

Governance transitions that move too fast create the very disruptions they're meant to prevent. By sequencing stakeholder alignment ahead of structural change, the organization absorbed a major operational shift without a single funding milestone slipping or a compliance gap emerging during the transition.

Value Delivered
  • Unified governance framework established across all consortium sites
  • Regulatory risk contained during a high-stakes funding transition
  • Compliance consistency improved across previously siloed oversight structures
  • Phased roadmap delivered, supporting long-term scalability
  • Leadership alignment achieved before — not during — execution
  • Operational continuity maintained with zero milestone disruptions

The cost of misaligned governance isn't visible until something fails. The value of this work was measured in what didn't happen: no audit findings, no funding delays, no operational breakdown during a period when any of those outcomes was plausible.

Operational Design
The Cost of Invisible Demand: Building Intake Governance to Protect Delivery Capacity
The Business Problem

When leadership can approve work without visibility into what it costs to deliver it, organizations consistently overcommit. In this coordinating center, work was entering execution through informal channels — email, hallway conversations, ad hoc requests — and bypassing any feasibility review before leadership sign-off. The result was predictable: teams absorbed more work than they could absorb, projects launched without clear requirements, and rework became a constant drain on capacity. Staff who recognized the problem didn't raise it, because there was no neutral mechanism for doing so without risk to the relationship. The organization needed a system that made the true cost of commitments visible before those commitments were made.

What Was at Stake

Team capacity, delivery quality, and the organization's ability to sustain execution velocity as program volume grew. Unchecked, the pattern would have compounded — more overcommitment, more rework, higher staff attrition risk.

The Intervention

Designed and implemented a structured intake and feasibility governance framework. Standardized how work requests entered the system, established a mandatory feasibility checkpoint before leadership commitment, and built in change management strategies that normalized clarity-seeking without creating organizational friction.

Why It Mattered

Demand management is a leadership issue, not a process issue. The framework worked because it gave leadership better information — not less authority. By making capacity, timelines, and tradeoffs visible before approval, decisions improved and delivery teams could execute against commitments that were actually achievable.

Value Delivered
  • Informal project starts and undocumented approvals eliminated
  • Feasibility review embedded as a standard pre-commitment step
  • Scope, timeline, and resourcing clarity established at intake
  • Rework driven by incomplete requirements materially reduced
  • Staff empowered to raise feasibility concerns through a neutral process
  • Leadership gained real-time visibility into workload and capacity

The most expensive projects are the ones that enter execution before anyone understands what they actually require. This framework made the invisible costs of overcommitment visible — and gave leadership the information to make better decisions before those costs were incurred.

Organizational Resilience
When Funding Stops but Obligations Don't: Managing Workforce Continuity and Contract Protection During a Federal Disruption
The Business Problem

A federal funding disruption does not suspend an organization's contractual obligations — it exposes them. When a government shutdown forced the furlough of a department's research unit, it created a problem that went well beyond temporary operational slowdown. Staff who had been formally named as committed personnel in a separately awarded, active federal contract were now displaced. That contract had defined deliverable requirements and a prescreening phase that could not launch without those specific individuals. The organization faced three simultaneous risks: contractual non-performance on a live federal engagement, loss of institutional knowledge during an extended displacement period, and no structured plan for returning staff to productive contribution when funding resumed. Treating these as sequential problems — address the furlough, then address the contract, then address re-onboarding — would have compounded the exposure at each step. All three had to be resolved as a single coordinated problem.

What Was at Stake

The organization's ability to fulfill named-personnel obligations on an active federal contract, preserve institutional knowledge across two programs during displacement, and return to full operational capacity without losing ground on either engagement when funding resumed.

The Intervention

Served as primary strategic partner to executive leadership throughout the disruption — and as the sole remaining unit member — simultaneously maintaining operational continuity and leading personnel planning at the organizational level. In partnership with the Executive Director and Department Administrator, assessed all active commitments to prioritize personnel return by contractual obligation and institutional need, built the return justification and sequencing plan, and designed a structured interim work scope — a targeted operational audit of call center performance, KPI analysis, workflow efficiency, and data collection quality — that gave returning staff immediate productive contribution from day one. Identified the active federal contract as the natural transition point, connecting displaced personnel directly to the engagement that had already committed them by name.

Why It Mattered

Organizational disruptions create compounding risk precisely because they arrive as multiple problems simultaneously. The intervention worked because it was designed as a portfolio-level response — workforce planning, interim productivity, and contract protection treated as one coordinated strategy rather than three separate recovery efforts. The result was an organization that resumed at full capacity without a gap in contractual performance or institutional knowledge.

Value Delivered
  • Named personnel preserved in active federal contract, protecting the organization's ability to meet DOD deliverable obligations
  • Returning staff entered productive contribution immediately through structured audit work — no re-onboarding lag
  • Interim audit produced actionable operational intelligence: workflow improvements, eCRF standards, data collection benchmarks
  • Federal contract prescreening phase launched on schedule, fulfilling SOW requirements that were at direct risk
  • Primary program operational continuity maintained throughout the disruption period
  • Institutional knowledge retained across both programs during full-unit displacement

A funding disruption is a stress test of how well an organization understands its own obligations. The organizations that recover fastest are the ones that treat workforce continuity, contractual protection, and operational readiness as a single problem — not a sequence of three.

Technology Strategy
Before You Implement, Assess: Building an Evidence-Based AI Adoption Strategy in a Regulated Multi-Team Environment
The Business Problem

AI adoption in regulated environments fails in a specific and predictable way: it is driven by enthusiasm rather than evidence. Staff begin using AI tools independently and inconsistently, compliance risk accumulates invisibly across teams with different regulatory obligations, and leadership — lacking visibility into actual usage patterns — makes implementation decisions based on assumption rather than data. By the time the gaps surface, the organization has already committed to a rollout it cannot safely or sustainably support. For this department operating across multiple regulated research programs, that pattern was already in motion. Staff across regulatory, clinical, data, and technical functions were engaging with AI tools in ways leadership could not see and had not assessed. Before a formal implementation strategy could be responsibly designed, the organization needed a grounded, evidence-based picture of where it actually stood.

What Was at Stake

Regulatory standing under institutional and federal standards, the credibility of any AI implementation strategy built without an accurate baseline, and the organization's ability to sequence adoption in a way that built staff confidence rather than resistance — across a workforce with widely varying technical literacy and compliance exposure.

The Intervention

Designed and led a department-wide AI readiness assessment spanning 10 teams and approximately 40 staff across regulatory, clinical, data, and technical functions. Developed a diagnostic survey instrument capturing current usage patterns, experience levels, comfort, and compliance considerations specific to each team type. Analyzed results to identify patterns, common use cases, literacy gaps, and risk exposure by function. Delivered a structured executive briefing — including results analysis, prioritized implementation recommendations grounded in the data, strategic opportunity identification, and a phased adoption roadmap — enabling leadership to make informed, defensible decisions. Simultaneously led hands-on AI adoption with an immediate team, deploying Microsoft Copilot with compliance training embedded from day one, establishing a replicable model for broader rollout.

Why It Mattered

An AI readiness assessment is not a preliminary step — it is a risk management decision. Without one, organizations implement based on what they assume about staff behavior and compliance exposure. With one, they implement based on what is actually true. The difference between those two starting points determines whether adoption builds capability or creates liability.

Value Delivered
  • Executive leadership initiated AI implementation directly informed by assessment findings, including deployment of AI-enabled tools on the department's public-facing platform
  • Delivered a prioritized, compliance-aware implementation roadmap — giving leadership a defensible, data-grounded starting point
  • Surfaced strategic AI opportunities previously invisible to leadership due to lack of cross-team usage visibility
  • Immediate team successfully onboarded to Microsoft Copilot with compliance training embedded, establishing a replicable rollout model
  • Assessment framework designed for reuse across future technology adoption initiatives
  • Leadership positioned to sequence adoption by risk level and team readiness — rather than deploying uniformly and managing fallout

The question is never whether an organization should adopt AI. The question is whether leadership has the evidence to do it responsibly. A structured readiness assessment transforms AI adoption from an organizational gamble into a sequenced, risk-informed strategy — one that builds confidence across the workforce rather than compliance exposure underneath it.

Building From Zero: Operational Infrastructure Design and Compliant Program Launch for a Growth-Stage Behavioral Health Organization
The Business Problem

Growth-stage organizations in behavioral health face a problem that is structurally different from most other industries: the regulatory bar for launching a new service line is not a function of organizational size — it is fixed, and it does not move for organizations that are not yet ready. A behavioral health agency preparing to launch a Multisystemic Therapy program faced this problem directly. MST is a structured, evidence-based intervention for youth with serious behavioral and legal involvement, delivered under contract with the Department of Juvenile Justice. To receive referrals, the agency needed to demonstrate that it had the staffing, documentation infrastructure, compliance posture, and stakeholder relationships to deliver the program at the standard DJJ requires — before a single client was served. The organization had the clinical intent and the contract opportunity. What it lacked was the operational infrastructure to execute against either. That infrastructure did not exist and needed to be built from the ground up, on a live launch timeline, with no margin for compliance gaps.

What Was at Stake

The organization's ability to activate a government-contracted service line on schedule, maintain compliance with DBHDS workforce documentation requirements, establish the external referral relationships required to sustain program operations at launch, and build the internal infrastructure capable of supporting ongoing delivery — not just initial standup.

The Intervention

Engaged as Fractional Director of Strategy and Operations through ZenStrata Consulting LLC. Designed and implemented a complete workforce infrastructure from the ground up — employee handbook, standardized offer letter templates, and a comprehensive onboarding checklist built to DBHDS compliance requirements for employee file documentation. Served as project lead for the full MST program launch across all phases: position scoping, candidate pipeline development, staff hiring and offer execution, contract execution, and stakeholder alignment. Coordinated directly with the Department of Juvenile Justice to establish referral pipelines and the external partnership infrastructure required to sustain program operations at and beyond launch. Managed ongoing cross-functional coordination between MST clinical staff and agency leadership to ensure operational alignment and program readiness.

Why It Mattered

A program launch in a regulated human services environment is not primarily a project management challenge — it is a compliance design challenge. The sequencing of workforce infrastructure, credentialing, stakeholder relationships, and external contract activation must be precise, because regulators and referral partners assess readiness simultaneously and do not distinguish between organizational immaturity and organizational unwillingness to comply. By designing the infrastructure to meet the standard before it was tested, the organization was positioned to launch with confidence rather than exposure.

Value Delivered
  • Complete workforce and HR infrastructure built from zero — employee handbook, offer templates, and DBHDS-compliant onboarding documentation
  • MST program launch on track for July 2026 — on time and on scope against a government-contracted delivery timeline
  • Full MST clinical and administrative team hired through a managed recruitment and offer execution process
  • Referral pipeline established with the Department of Juvenile Justice, building the external partnership infrastructure required to sustain operations at launch
  • Ongoing cross-functional coordination maintained between clinical staff and agency leadership, sustaining program readiness through the pre-launch period
  • Audit-ready hiring and onboarding practices embedded as standard operating infrastructure — not a one-time standup effort

In regulated human services, the question is never whether you can deliver the program — it is whether you can demonstrate, before delivery begins, that you have built the infrastructure to do it responsibly. Organizations that treat compliance as a design constraint from day one launch with credibility. Organizations that treat it as a checklist to complete at the end launch with exposure.
